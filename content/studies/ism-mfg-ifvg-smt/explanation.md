ISM Manufacturing drops at 10:00 ET on the first business day of the month. Above 50 means expansion, below 50 means contraction — either way, the number moves the Nasdaq.

This study uses the release-bar high and low as targets. Price sweeps one side, leaves an Inverse Fair Value Gap (IFVG) on the rejection, and you enter when it breaks back inside the range, aiming for the opposite side's liquidity. At 10:00 ET the opening range is already established, so liquidity pools form cleanly above and below the release bar. An optional S&P 500 (ES) filter only keeps the trade when the S&P confirms the same move. Tested on 1-minute Nasdaq 100 (NQ) data, 2016 to 2026.

Rules: sweep the 10:00 ET candle high/low, wait for the rejection gap, enter on the break back inside. Stop one tick beyond the sweep. Target the opposite liquidity (the release-bar pivot). The ES filter keeps a trade only if the S&P reaches its mirror target within the 10:01–12:00 ET window.

### Why the filter matters

The Nasdaq and the S&P move together almost all the time. When the Nasdaq hits its target but the S&P doesn't follow, it's one index drifting alone — usually a fakeout, not a real reversal. Demanding S&P confirmation drops those and keeps the setups where both indices turn together.
