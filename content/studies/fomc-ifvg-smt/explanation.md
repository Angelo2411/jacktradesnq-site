FOMC hits at 14:00 ET. The release bar sends price one way, sweeps the stops, then reverses — the real direction comes second.

This study uses the release-bar high and low as targets on Nasdaq 100 (NQ). Price runs one side, leaves an Inverse Fair Value Gap (IFVG) on the rejection, and you enter when it breaks back inside the range, aiming for the opposite side's liquidity. An optional S&P 500 (ES) filter only keeps the trade when the S&P confirms the same move, so you're not fading a lone index. Tested on 1-minute NQ data, 2016 to 2026.

Rules: sweep the 14:00 ET candle high/low, wait for the rejection gap, enter on the break back inside. Stop one tick beyond the sweep. Target the opposite liquidity (the release-bar pivot). The ES filter keeps a trade only if the S&P reaches its mirror target within the 14:01–17:00 ET window.

### Performance — NQ, 10 years

Without SMT: 35 trades, profit factor 1.26, net +104 NQ points. With SMT: 26 trades, profit factor 1.92, net +241 NQ points ("TP only" variant).

### Why the filter matters

The Nasdaq and the S&P move together almost all the time. When the Nasdaq hits its target but the S&P doesn't follow, it's one index drifting alone — usually a fakeout, not a real reversal. Demanding S&P confirmation drops those and keeps the setups where both indices turn together. FOMC volatility shifted hard post-2021; the release candle average range jumped from 17 to 180 points.

### Disclaimer

26 filtered trades is a thin sample, so treat this as a read on the setup, not a guarantee. AI-assisted analysis, not financial advice.
