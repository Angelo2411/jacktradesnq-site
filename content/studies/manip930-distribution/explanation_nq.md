The 09:30–10:00 ET manipulation is a real phenomenon: price sweeps a key reference level and immediately rejects — a textbook liquidity grab. On NQ futures, this sweep-reject pattern fires on **61.9% of all trading days** (1,581 of 2,553 days, 2016–2026). That's a high-frequency behavior — it is not rare.

But the question that matters is: *does the 10:00–11:00 distribution follow through in the direction of the rejection?*

**No — it does not.** The distribution follows through just **51.6%** of the time. That is indistinguishable from a coin flip. The average post-sweep move is **−0.2 points** — effectively flat. The average favorable excursion (how far price travels in the right direction before reversing or fading) is **43.5 points**, which looks tempting — but the closing result after one hour is noise.

NQ's high manipulation rate makes it *feel* tradable. The sweep is visible, the rejection is sharp, and the move often looks like it's going to run. But the data says: it doesn't. The distribution window is random, not systematic.

This is an observational finding: the 09:30 manipulation is frequent, but the 10:00 distribution is not a reliable continuation. Treat NQ's post-sweep behavior as a coin flip, not a signal.
